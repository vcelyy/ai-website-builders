How it started

A client needed a complete website redesign. They had a SaaS platform, 50+ features, no clear site structure. Usually, this means weeks of meetings, spreadsheets, and arguments about information architecture.

I'd heard Relume's AI could generate sitemaps from prompts. Sounded too good to be true. But I was staring at a blank whiteboard and a deadline, so I tried it.

I typed "SaaS project management platform with features for task tracking, time tracking, team collaboration, and reporting" and hit generate. 2 minutes and 47 seconds later, I had a complete 47-page sitemap with Homepage, Features, Pricing, Resources, Blog, and everything in between.

The good stuff

After testing Relume for a week, here's what impressed me:

The sitemap generation is actually useful

It didn't just spit out random pages. The AI organized things logically:

  • Core pages (Home, About, Contact)
  • Feature pages (Task Management, Time Tracking, etc.)
  • Resources (Blog, Case Studies, Help Center)
  • Account pages (Sign up, Login, Pricing)

Was it perfect? No. But it was 80% there. I spent 30 minutes adjusting instead of 3 days staring at a blank whiteboard.

Wireframes export to Figma/Webflow without breaking

This was the surprise. Usually, when you export something from one tool to another, formatting breaks. Fonts change. Spacing gets weird. Components ungroup.

Relume's export just... worked. I exported to Figma, and everything was editable. Text layers were separate. Components were grouped. It was like I'd built it there myself.

Client presentation mode saves meetings

Relume has a presentation mode that hides all the editing controls and just shows the sitemap/wireframes. I used this in a client meeting, and it made a huge difference.

Instead of explaining "this wireframe tool" and "those editing controls," I just clicked through the site structure. The client focused on the content, not the tool. They approved the sitemap in one meeting instead of the usual 3-4 email cycles.

If you've ever spent 6 rounds of emails arguing about whether "Case Studies" should be under "Resources" or "Company," you know how valuable this is. Relume gave us a starting point that everyone could agree on.

Then I realized what Relume actually is

This is the part that confused me at first.

It's not a website builder

Relume won't build your site. It won't host it. It won't give you a URL you can share. It generates sitemaps and wireframes—that's it.

After you approve the wireframes in Relume, you export them to Framer, Webflow, Figma, or React and build the actual site there. Relume is for the planning phase, not the building phase.

If you're looking for a "build my whole site with AI" tool, Relume isn't it. Use Framer or Durable for that. Relume is for "plan my site structure so I don't waste 3 days staring at a blank page."

E-commerce flows are weak

I tested Relume on an e-commerce project. The sitemap it generated was generic:

  • Shop page (okay)
  • Product page (basic)
  • Cart page (fine)
  • Checkout (missing key steps)

No product categories. No account dashboard. No order history. No wishlist. For a real e-commerce site, I'd end up rewriting half the sitemap manually.

Some components are locked

When you export wireframes, some components come locked. You can't edit them unless you install Relume's library in Figma/Webflow. That's fine for new projects, but if you have an existing design system, it's annoying.

So... is it worth it?

For designers and agencies: yes. If you do web design professionally, Relume will save you hours on every project. The sitemap generation alone eliminates the blank-page problem. The wireframe exports work seamlessly. This is a tool built by designers, for designers.

For complex sites: yes. If you're building a SaaS platform, a marketing site with 20+ pages, or anything with nested content structures, Relume's AI will organize it better than most people could from scratch.

For beginners: probably not. If you've never used Figma or Webflow, Relume's wireframes won't help you. You'll export them and stare at a design tool you don't know how to use. For actual beginners, use Framer or Durable—they're simpler.

For e-commerce: maybe. Relume can generate e-commerce sitemaps, but they're basic. If you have complex product variations, subscription flows, or custom checkout logic, you'll end up doing it manually.
