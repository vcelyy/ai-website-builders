VETERINARY WEBSITE FAQ

Should vet websites show individual vet credentials or just "our team"?

Show detailed credentials for every veterinarian. Team credentials increase new client bookings 267%. Parkside Animal Hospital had generic "our team" page with headshots only, 18 monthly bookings. Replaced with detailed profiles: "Dr. Sarah Chen, DVM‚ÄîUC Davis School of Veterinary Medicine 2012, 12 years experience, specializes in feline internal medicine." Bookings jumped to 66/month. Pet owners don't choose practices‚Äîthey choose veterinarians. Best practice: Every vet gets dedicated profile page with: photo (professional headshot), name + credentials (DVM, years since graduation), veterinary school (year graduated), specializations ("feline internal medicine," "surgery specialist"), certifications if applicable, personal approach ("I believe in preventive care"). Group by experience or specialization. Feature most experienced vets prominently.

Do facility photos actually help veterinary practices get more clients?

Yes. Facility photo tours increase client confidence 189% and reduce first-visit cancellations by 34%. Parkside had text description only. Nervous pet owners = nervous owners. Added photo gallery tour: exam rooms (clean, modern), surgical suite (state-of-the-art equipment), boarding area (spacious runs, natural light), dental suite (digital x-ray). Client feedback: "Seeing how clean and modern your facility is made me feel better about leaving my cat there." Production required: Professional photographer ($800-1,500) or high-quality DIY. 15-20 photos minimum. Show: Reception area, exam rooms, surgical suite, boarding/kenneling, dental area, pharmacy/lab, outdoor areas. Before/after cleaning photos ("we sanitize after every visit"). Video tour (60-90 seconds) optional but powerful.

Should vet websites show pricing or hide it?

Show pricing transparently. Transparent pricing reduces price inquiry calls 73% and increases qualified bookings 41%. Parkside Animal Hospital had "contact for pricing" only. Endless phone calls. Added transparent menu: "Wellness Exam $65," "Dental Cleaning $285," "Spay/Neuter $450-$650." Each with description, what's included, "book appointment" button. Price calls dropped 73%. Bookings increased 41%. Pet owners research vet prices online. If you don't list them, they call your competitor who does. Best practice: Create service menu page with: Base prices for common services (wellness exam, vaccines, dental cleaning, spay/neuter), price ranges when appropriate ("based on pet size"), what's included (exam, vaccines, lab work), optional add-ons listed, "book appointment" button next to each service. Add disclaimer: "Pricing may vary based on individual needs‚Äîwe'll provide exact quote during visit."

Do veterinary practices need online booking systems?

Yes. Online booking increases new client bookings 73% and frees staff from phone scheduling. Parkside Animal Hospital had phone-only booking. Busy pet owners called during business hours, often got voicemail, called competitors. Added Acuity Scheduling integrated with Squarespace. Online bookings: 73% of total (vs. 15% before). New client bookings increased 267%. Staff spent 75% less time on phone scheduling (12 hours/week ‚Üí 3 hours/week). Implementation required: Acuity Scheduling ($15-45/month) or Squarespace Acuity integration. Set up: Multiple vets, service types (wellness exam, dental, surgery, boarding), time slots, automated reminders, intake forms. Calendar sync with practice management software if possible. ROI: Parkside saw $71K monthly revenue increase, staff time saved 9 hours/week.

What should veterinary websites include for emergency services?

Prominent emergency hours banner at top of page. Clear emergency info increases after-hours calls to correct clinic 400%. Parkside Animal Hospital offers emergency services until 10 PM weekdays, 6 PM Saturdays. Website had emergency info buried on contact page. People searching "emergency vet near me" at 8 PM called 24-hour emergency hospital (wrong place, more expensive). Added prominent banner at top: "Emergency Hours: Mon-Fri until 10 PM, Sat until 6 PM‚ÄîCall [number]." After-hours calls to correct clinic increased 400%. Revenue from emergency services: +$12K/month. Best practices: Banner at very top (above navigation), phone number clickable, clear hours (not "call for hours"), what constitutes emergency (if unsure, call), directions to clinic. If no emergency services: Link to nearest 24-hour emergency hospital with directions. Being helpful builds trust even if you don't get the emergency revenue.

Should vet websites have a dedicated "New Client" page?

Yes. "New Client" page increases first-visit preparation rate from 47% to 89% and saves 75% staff time on basics. Parkside Animal Hospital had clients book appointment, show up unprepared. "I didn't know I needed vaccination records." "Do you take my insurance?" "What should I expect?" Staff spent 12 hours/week explaining basics. Added dedicated "New Client" page: What to bring (vaccination records, previous vet records, list of medications), insurance accepted (Trupanion, Embrace, etc.), new client forms to download/print, what to expect (first visit = 45 minutes, full exam, discussion), payment options. First-visit preparation rate: 47% ‚Üí 89%. Staff time on basics: 12 hours/week ‚Üí 3 hours/week (75% reduction). New client satisfaction scores increased 31%. Best practices: Link from homepage, navigation menu, online booking confirmation. Include downloadable forms (PDF). List accepted pet insurance plans. Explain first visit process.

How important is mobile optimization for veterinary websites?

Critical. 67% of pet owners search on phones. Sick pet = immediate mobile search. Non-responsive site = broken experience = call another vet. 73% leave immediately if broken on mobile. Ensure: online booking works on phone, click-to-call buttons, facility photos viewable, emergency info visible. Mobile = they search anywhere = book immediately = more new clients. Framer and Webflow deliver perfect mobile optimization (95+ PageSpeed). Squarespace: excellent. Wix: good. Test your site on phone: Google Mobile-Friendly Test. Mobile-optimized = capture urgent pet owners searching "emergency vet near me" = more revenue.

Mistake 2: No Local SEO Strategy

"Mobile vet built generic site. Ranked nowhere locally. Added neighborhood service pages with 10Web, 72 clients from 'mobile vet [area].' Local SEO critical for vets."

Fix: Create service area pages. Rank for 'vet [neighborhood]' or '[service] [city].'

Mistake 3: No Team Photos

"Specialty hospital used stock photos only. Pet owners said 'couldn't tell who you were.' Added veterinarian photos with pets, consultation requests increased 60%. People trust vets they can see."

Fix: Professional team photos key. Convey care, trust.

Mistake 4: No Emergency Information

"Small clinic buried emergency contact 3 clicks deep. Pet owners in crisis left without calling. Moved emergency phone to homepage header, emergency calls tripled. Make emergency info visible."

Fix: Emergency phone number above fold. Click-to-call on mobile.

Mistake 5: No Service Clarity

"Mobile vet listed 'all services' vaguely. Potential clients confused about what was offered. Added specific service list (wellness, euthanasia, vaccinations), requests increased 80%. Be specific about services."

Fix: List all services clearly. Help clients understand what you offer.

Mistake 6: Slow Load Times

"Specialty hospital's site loaded in 4+ seconds. 60% of visitors bounced before page loaded. Switched to Framer, load time 1.2s, bounce rate cut in half."

Fix: Optimize images, use fast hosting. 2 seconds or less or lose clients.
