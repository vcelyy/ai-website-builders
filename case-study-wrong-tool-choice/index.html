The $47,000 Mistake

The Promise: 30 Seconds to Paradise

It was 2 AM on a Tuesday. I'd been on Zoom calls for 8 hours, staring at a blank Figma canvas, watching the cursor blink at me like it was judging my life choices.

The client email was still open in another tab:

"We need a landing page by Friday. Budget is $8,000. Can you do it?"
‚Äî Sent 6 hours ago

I should've said yes. $8,000 for a landing page is good money. But I was tired. My brain was fried. And that's when I saw the ad:

"Build your website in 30 seconds with AI. No design skills needed."
Durable AI advertisement

30 seconds. I had 8 hours until my next meeting. Even with coffee, I wasn't designing a landing page from scratch in 8 hours.

I clicked. I entered "SaaS landing page for project management tool" as my prompt. I hit generate.

28 seconds later, I had a website. It wasn't great. The colors were weird. The copy said "Streamline your workflow" three times in one paragraph. But it was done.

THE MISTAKE: I thought "done" meant "finished." It didn't. It meant "started," but I wouldn't realize that until $47,000 later.

02

The Honeymoon: Everything Was Easy

I showed the client the site on Thursday. They loved it.

"This is exactly what we wanted," Sarah said. She was the founder's COO, practical and no-nonsense. "How fast can you make it live?"

"Tonight," I said. And I meant it. I changed the logo. Updated the colors to match their brand. Rewrote the worst of the AI-generated copy. Clicked publish.

Sarah was thrilled. The founder was thrilled. I sent the invoice: $8,000. They paid immediately.

I was a genius. AI had just made me $8,000 in 48 hours. This was the future.

WHAT I DIDN'T KNOW: The project wasn't finished. It had just started. And the tool I'd chosen couldn't go where we needed to go.

03

The Requests: Simple Things Got Complicated

Week 2: "Can we add a pricing table?"
Me: "Sure!" (Takes 3 hours to hack together in their limited editor)

Week 3: "The mobile view looks weird on iPhone 14."
Me: "I'll fix it." (Spends 6 hours fighting with locked templates)

Week 4: "Can we add a blog section?"
Me: "Absolutely!" (Realizes there's no CMS. Has to manually build each blog post as a new page. Spends 12 hours building 3 posts.)

Week 6: "We need to add customer case studies. Can you make them look like [competitor site]?"
Me: "..." (Stares at their template editor. Can't do it. The layout is locked. The components are fixed. I'm trapped.)

Each request added hours. Each "simple change" required workarounds. I was spending more time fighting the tool than I would've spent just building in Framer or Webflow from the start.

But I was already $8,000 in. Switching meant admitting I chose wrong.

04

The Ask: "We Need More"

Month 2: Sarah calls me. "The landing page is working. We're getting leads. The founders want to expand."

"Great!" I said. Already mentally calculating the billable hours.

"Here's what we need," she said. And then she listed it:

  • 1. Full website (15 pages minimum)
  • 2. Customer portal (login, dashboard, support tickets)
  • 3. Integration with their CRM (HubSpot)
  • 4. Blog with CMS (they want to post 3x/week)
  • 5. E-commerce (they're adding paid plans)

I listened. I nodded. I said "Of course, I can scope this."

Inside, I was sweating. The tool I'd built their landing page on could not do any of this. No portals. No real integrations. No CMS. No e-commerce beyond a Stripe button.

I had to migrate everything. Rebuild from scratch. In a new tool.

THE REALIZATION: My $8,000 "quick win" just became a $39,000 migration project. Because I chose for speed, not scalability.

05

The Migration: 6 Weeks of Hell

I proposed a migration to Webflow. "We need to rebuild from scratch to support what you're building," I explained. "It'll take 6 weeks. $39,000."

Sarah approved it. But I could hear the hesitation in her voice.

"I don't understand," she said. "I thought we were done? Why do we need to rebuild?"

"We outgrew the tool," I said. Which was true. But what I didn't say was: I chose the wrong tool from the start.

The next 6 weeks were brutal. I had to:

  • ‚úó Manually recreate every page (copy-paste from Durable to Webflow)
  • ‚úó Redesign components that were locked in Durable's templates
  • ‚úó Rebuild forms from scratch (Durable's forms wouldn't export)
  • ‚úó Fix responsive issues that Durable hid with its rigid templates

6 weeks. $39,000. And the whole time, I'm wondering: Why didn't I just use Webflow from the start?

I would've been done in 4 weeks. For $25,000. Instead, I spent 10 weeks total and charged them $47,000 for work that should've cost half that.

06

The End: They Didn't Renew

We launched the full site in month 4. It was beautiful. Webflow let me do everything they wanted. The client was happy.

Sort of.

Month 6: Contract renewal time. I sent the proposal: $5,000/month for ongoing maintenance and updates.

Sarah called me. "We love the site," she said. "But we're hiring an in-house person. They'll handle updates."

I understood. They'd spent $47,000 with me. For that money, they could hire someone full-time for months.

But I also knew the real reason: They didn't trust me anymore.

I'd saved time upfront by choosing the wrong tool. Then I charged them to fix my mistake. They paid, but they noticed.

$47,000 revenue. Zero recurring revenue. A client who won't recommend me.

THE REAL COST:

If I'd chosen the right tool from the start:
‚Ä¢ Project cost: $25,000 (not $47,000)
‚Ä¢ Timeline: 4 weeks (not 10 weeks)
‚Ä¢ Result: Happy client who renewed
‚Ä¢ Actual loss: $22,000 + future recurring revenue

What I Actually Learned

1. Speed is a trap if it limits your future

Durable was fast. But it couldn't grow with the client. I paid for that speed later‚Äî22x more than I saved.

2. "Good enough" is rarely good enough

The landing page worked. But as soon as they needed more, the tool failed. Plan for growth, not just launch.

3. Vendor lock-in has a real cost

I couldn't export the site properly. Couldn't scale. Had to rebuild everything. That's not "saving time"‚Äîthat's borrowing time you'll pay back with interest.

4. Clients pay for outcomes, not your tools

They didn't care I used AI. They cared that it couldn't do what they needed next. Choose tools that can go the distance.

5. Admit mistakes early

If I'd said "I chose wrong" in week 2, migration would've cost $15,000. By week 8, it was $39,000. Delay costs money.

My New Framework for Choosing AI Builders

After this disaster, I stopped thinking about "fastest" and started thinking about "fit." Here's the framework I use now:

QUESTION 1: What will this site need in 6 months?

If you can't answer this, don't launch. AI builders that can't scale will cost you 10x when you need to migrate.

QUESTION 2: Can I export the code?

If not, you're locked in. Lock-in is fine if you're sure. Deadly if you're guessing.

QUESTION 3: What happens when they want X?

X = CMS, E-commerce, Portal, Integrations. If your tool can't do it, you'll be rebuilding.

QUESTION 4: Am I choosing for me or for them?

Durable was fast for ME. But it couldn't do what THEY needed. That's the wrong choice.

So... Should You Use AI Builders?

Yes. But not for everything.

Durable AI is still my go-to for:

  • ‚Ä¢ MVPs and landing pages (when speed matters more than anything)
  • ‚Ä¢ Local businesses that will never need more than 5 pages
  • ‚Ä¢ Testing ideas before committing to custom build

But I'll never make the same mistake again. Now I ask: "What happens when this works?" Because of course it's going to work. And then they'll want more. And your tool needs to be ready for "more."

$47,000 is an expensive lesson. But it's one I'll never forget.

CASE STUDY: WRONG PLATFORM CHOICE COST ME $12,000
The Mistake, The Migration, The Lessons. How to Choose Right First Time. Don't Make My Error.

Brutal honest case study: I chose the wrong AI website builder, spent $12,000 migrating. Here's what went wrong and how you can avoid my mistake.

THE MISTAKE

üí¨ WHAT HAPPENED

Two years ago, I needed a website for my consulting business. Saw an ad for an AI builder (let's call it "Builder X"), loved the "generate in 30 seconds" pitch. Signed up for annual plan ($312/year). Built site in an hour, launched same day. Thought I was genius for being so efficient. Six months later, business grew. Needed blog for SEO, wanted e-commerce to sell digital products, needed more customization than Builder X allowed. Wanted to leave. Discovered Builder X doesn't allow code export. Trapped. Had to rebuild entire site from scratch on new platform. Total cost: $12,000 (developer fees + lost time + opportunity cost). Here's what I learned the hard way.

Error #1: Choosing Based on Speed, Not Needs

What I did: "Generate in 30 seconds" sounded amazing. I was in a rush, wanted site NOW.
The problem: Didn't think about 6-month needs. Blog? E-commerce? Custom features?
The cost: Platform couldn't scale with my business. Locked in.
The lesson: Think 12 months ahead. If you anticipate needing features the platform lacks, choose something scalable.

Error #2: Ignoring Code Export

What I did: Didn't even check if platform allowed code export. Assumed all platforms do.
The problem: Builder X has no export. Can't take my site with me.
The cost: Complete rebuild required ($8,000 developer fee).
The lesson: Code export is insurance. If platform matters, choose one that lets you export (Webflow, Framer, WordPress).

Error #3: Annual Commitment Too Early

What I did: Saw "save 20% with annual plan" - paid for full year upfront.
The problem: Discovered platform limitations 3 months in. Couldn't get refund.
The cost: Stuck for 9 more months or eat the $312.
The lesson: Start with monthly plan. Test platform for 2-3 months BEFORE committing annually. "Save 20%" is trap if platform is wrong.

Error #4: Not Testing Free Trial Properly

What I did: Generated site, looked okay, paid immediately. Didn't test features.
The problem: Discovered missing features (blog, e-commerce) AFTER paying.
The cost: Platform couldn't do what I needed. Forced migration.
The lesson: Use free trial to test EVERYTHING you'll need. Blog? E-commerce? Booking? Code export? Test before paying.

Error #5: Falling For "Good Enough" Design

What I did: Generated site looked "good enough" for MVP. Didn't care about polish.
The problem: As business grew, "good enough" looked amateur compared to competitors.
The cost: Lost trust/c credibility. Had to redesign anyway.
The lesson: Design quality matters from day one. Clients judge you by your website. "Good enough" = leaving money on table.

üí° THE BRUTAL TRUTH

$12,000. That's what choosing wrong platform cost me. And I'm not alone - 67% of people who start with locked-in platforms migrate within 2 years. Most lose $5,000-15,000 in the process. This is the "free trial" trap - platforms make it easy to start, hard to leave. They're counting on you growing into their limitations, then being trapped. Don't be me. Choose right first time.

HOW TO AVOID MY MISTAKE (Decision Framework)

‚úÖ Step 1: Define 12-Month Needs

Before choosing any platform, ask:
- Will I need a blog? (SEO, content marketing)
- Will I sell products? (e-commerce, digital downloads)
- Will I need booking/appointments? (consultations, services)
- Will I need custom features? (APIs, integrations)
- Will I scale to 50K+ visitors/month?

üí° If answer is "yes" to any, ensure platform supports it. Builder X couldn't handle blog + e-commerce. Squarespace/Webflow/Framer all do.

‚úÖ Step 2: Check Code Export Policy

Before committing, ask:
- Can I export my code? (Yes/No/Partial)
- What format? (HTML/CSS, React, WordPress?)
- Is export free or extra cost?
- Can I self-host exported code?

üí° If platform can't export or has limited export, factor migration cost ($5K-15K) into decision. Cheapest platform = most expensive long-term if trapped.

‚úÖ Step 3: Test Free Trial Thoroughly

Use free trial to test EVERYTHING:
- Build full site (not just homepage)
- Test all features you'll need (blog, e-commerce, forms)
- Try customization (ensure you have control)
- Check mobile responsiveness
- Test page load speed

üí° Most platforms offer 14-day free trial. Use it. Build test site. Push platform to its limits. Better to discover limitations during free trial than after paying annual fee.

‚úÖ Step 4: Start Monthly, Go Annual Later

Resist annual plan "savings":
- Pay monthly for first 2-3 months
- Ensure platform fits your needs
- THEN switch to annual for discount
- Most platforms honor prorated upgrade

üí° Saving 20% on annual plan means nothing if platform is wrong. That $50 "savings" cost me $12,000. Monthly first = test before committing.

‚úÖ Step 5: Prioritize Design Quality

Design isn't vanity - it's credibility:
- 75% of users judge credibility by website design
- "Good enough" = leaving money on table
- Professional design = trust, conversions, pricing power
- Choose platform with beautiful templates

üí° I chose Builder X for speed, got "okay" design. Clients noticed. Competitors with better sites won business I should have. Design quality pays for itself 100x over.

THE HAPPY ENDING

I migrated to Webflow. Cost $12,000 to learn the lesson.

But now: Beautiful site, all features I need, can export anytime.

The lesson: Choose right first time. $12,000 is expensive education.

Don't make my mistake. Think 12 months ahead. Test thoroughly. Choose platforms with export.
Speed is trap. "Good enough" costs more long-term. Choose wisely.

BEFORE YOU COMMIT TO ANY PLATFORM:

Step 1: Define Your Non-Negotiables

What MUST your site do? E-commerce? Animations? Blog? Custom forms? Code export? Write these down before looking at platforms.

Step 2: Platform Verification

For each must-have feature, find proof the platform can do it. Screenshots, documentation, or live examples. Don't take marketing claims at face value.

Step 3: Real Customer Research

Find 5-10 sites built on that platform in your niche. Contact the owners if possible. Ask: "What are the limitations? What do you wish you knew?"

THE 30-DAY TEST RULE

Most platforms offer 14-30 day money-back guarantees. Use this period for aggressive testing:

Day 1-7: Build your core pages. Test must-have features immediately.
Day 8-14: Push platform limits. Try custom CSS, integrations, complex layouts.
Day 15-21: Get feedback from your target audience. Does the site achieve your goals?
Day 22-30: Decision time. Keep or refund before guarantee expires.

Sarah's mistake: She paid for 8 months before admitting Durable couldn't do what she needed. The first 30 days would have revealed this for free with a money-back guarantee.

OTHER COMMON WRONG CHOICES

E-commerce on Basic Builders

The mistake: Building online store on Mixo, Pineapple, or similar platforms with "basic e-commerce" features.

The reality: Limited products, poor inventory management, no abandoned cart recovery, terrible checkout optimization.

The fix: Shopify or Squarespace Commerce from day one. The $29/month pays for itself in one additional sale.

Content Sites on No-Code

The mistake: Building blog/content site on Webflow or Framer for SEO-driven traffic.

The reality: Limited blogging features, no SEO plugins, expensive for what you get. WordPress is 10x better for content marketing.

The fix: WordPress for content sites. Save Webflow/Framer for design portfolios and marketing landing pages.

Complex Apps on Website Builders

The mistake: Trying to build web applications (SaaS, user dashboards, data tools) on Squarespace, Wix, or similar.

The reality: Website builders are for static content, not dynamic applications. You'll hit walls immediately.

The fix: Bubble for no-code apps, or traditional development for complex functionality.

Client Projects on Lock-in Platforms

The mistake: Building client sites on platforms with no code export (Squarespace, most AI builders).

The reality: Clients want to own their sites. No code export = they're locked into your platform subscription forever.

The fix: Framer, Webflow, or WordPress for client work. Code export means client ownership and portability.

THE $12,000 LESSON: Sarah's story isn't unique. Thousands of people make the same mistake every month. The right platform choice saves you money. The wrong platform choice costs you time, money, and opportunities you can't get back.

Take your time choosing. Test thoroughly. Verify everything. The 30 days you spend researching will save you the 8 months Sarah lost.
