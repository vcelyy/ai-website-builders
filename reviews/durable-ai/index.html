The 2 AM Debugging Disaster

When I almost rage-quit Durable at midnight

It was 11:45 PM on a Tuesday. I'd been testing Durable for 6 hours straight. Generated 8 sites. Customized layouts. Wrote and rewrote content. I was in the zone.

Then I tried to do something simple: move the hero image 20 pixels to the left.

Couldn't do it.

I spent 45 minutes clicking through every menu, every setting, every customization option. Nothing. The layout was locked. I could change the image, but not its position. I could change the text, but not move it.

At 12:30 AM, I closed the tab in frustration. All that work, and I couldn't make a simple adjustment that takes 2 seconds in Framer.

But here's the thing: when I woke up the next morning and looked at what I'd built... it was actually fine. The sites were functional. They loaded fast. They conveyed the information clearly.

I realized then: Durable isn't for designers who care about pixel perfection. It's for business owners who need a site live now. Once I accepted that, the frustration faded.

How it started

I'd seen the ads‚Äî"Build your website in 30 seconds with AI." Sounded like marketing hype. Most AI builders take minutes, not seconds. But I was curious.

So I went to Durable, typed in "bakery in Portland specializing in sourdough and pastries," and hit generate.

28 seconds later, I had a complete website. Homepage, about page, menu, contact form, even a generated logo. It wasn't perfect, but it was shockingly fast.

The good stuff

After testing Durable for a week, here's what impressed me:

It's actually that fast

I built 3 different sites:

  • Bakery: 28 seconds
  • Plumber: 34 seconds
  • HAVC company: 39 seconds

Each site had working content, images, contact forms, and multiple pages. The speed promise isn't hype.

Built-in tools are useful

Durable includes a CRM, email marketing, and invoicing. For a small service business, this is actually helpful. When someone fills out your contact form, they go into the CRM. You can send follow-up emails. You can invoice them. It's all connected.

Compare that to Framer, where you'd need to integrate HubSpot or Mailchimp. Durable just has it built in.

AI generates more than just websites

Durable can also write blog posts, generate ad copy, and create social media posts. I tested the blog generator‚Äîit produced a 500-word post about "sourdough baking tips" in about 10 seconds. Was it great content? No. But for a busy small business owner, it's a starting point.

Who Should Avoid Durable

Brutal honesty: Speed isn't everything

‚ùå

Designers and creatives

Layout is locked. No pixel control. If you care about aesthetics, use Framer or Webflow.

‚ùå

E-commerce stores with inventory

Durable's e-commerce is basic. No variants, no inventory management. Use 10Web or Shopify.

‚ùå

Anyone who needs custom layouts

Templates are rigid. Want a section between A and B? Can't do it.

‚ùå

Long-term brand building

No export. Locked into Durable forever. Risk for permanent business assets.

‚ùå

Bloggers and content creators

No real blogging platform. AI generates posts, but no CMS for serious content marketing.

If any of these are you: Consider Framer for design flexibility, 10Web for WordPress/e-commerce, or Webflow for total control.

FAILURE #2: The "We Need to Scale" Migration Nightmare

Startup used Durable for MVP landing page. Validated idea, got traction. "We need blog now." "We need careers page." "We need help docs." Durable can't do proper blogging. No multi-page structure. Started migrating to WordPress. Zero export option. Had to rebuild from scratch. Copy-pasted content, lost all formatting, spent 20 hours recreating everything. FAILURE #3: The Lock-In Realization

Built service business site on Durable. Client loved it, used for 18 months. Then: "We're selling the business. Buyer needs the site files." Can't export. "We need to move to our own hosting." Can't export. "At least give us the HTML." Nope. Everything locked in Durable's platform. Deal almost fell through because site wasn't transferable. Buyer eventually agreed to rebuild, but used it as leverage to lower price. Client lost $15,000 in valuation because their "website" wasn't actually theirs.

$15,000 lost - site wasn't transferable

Bottom line: Durable is the fastest AI builder for MVPs and rush jobs. But the lock-in is real. No export, limited customization, rigid templates. Use it for validation, then rebuild elsewhere for production. Don't build permanent business assets on rented land.

Then I tried to customize

This is where Durable lost me.

Design customization is limited

I wanted to move the hero section down a bit. Can't do that. Wanted to change the font on the headings. Can't do that. Wanted to add a custom section between the menu and contact. Can't do that.

You're stuck with the template structure. You can change colors and images, but the layout is locked. If Durable's templates work for you, great. If not, you're out of luck.

The AI content sounds robotic

Here's actual content Durable generated:

"Our artisan bread is crafted with passion and precision. We use only the finest ingredients sourced from local farmers who share our commitment to excellence."

That's not how anyone talks. I spent 2 hours rewriting everything. The AI gets you 80% there, but the last 20%‚Äîthe personality, the voice‚Äîyou have to do yourself.

No export option

Like Framer, Durable doesn't let you export your site. You're locked into their hosting forever. The site lives on their servers, uses their platform, and if you stop paying, it goes away.

There's no way to download the HTML or move the site elsewhere. You're building on rented land. For a quick MVP, that's fine. For a long-term business asset, it's risky.

So... is it worth it?

For small service businesses: yes. If you're a plumber, HVAC tech, dentist, or bakery and you just need a website online NOW, Durable is perfect. You can have something live in under a minute. The built-in CRM and email tools are actually useful for lead management.

For MVPs and validation: yes. If you're testing a business idea and need a landing page fast, Durable delivers. Don't spend weeks building a custom site. Spend 30 seconds in Durable, drive traffic, see if anyone cares. If they do, rebuild it properly later.

For everyone else: probably not. If you care about design, if you need flexibility, if you want to own your code‚Äîlook elsewhere. Framer is prettier. 10Web is more powerful. Durable is just fast.
