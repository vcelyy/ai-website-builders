The Safe Choice That Holds Your Hand

Perfectly adequate. That's both praise and criticism.

Wix ADI is the Honda Civic of website builders. Reliable, ubiquitous, does everything adequately. You won't love it. You won't hate it. It'll just... work.

The good: Hand-holding for total beginners. 2-minute site generation. Every feature you could possibly want (event calendars, bookings, restaurants).

The bad: Your site will look like 10 million other Wix sites. Generic is baked in. Export is impossible—you're renting space on Wix's platform forever.

Choose Wix if: You need something online yesterday and don't care about standing out.
Avoid if: Your brand needs distinctiveness. You might want to leave Wix someday.

The "This Looks Like Every Other Wix Site" Moment

When I realized the tradeoff

It was Day 3 of testing Wix. I'd generated 7 different sites across different niches:

  • Yoga studio in Portland
  • SaaS startup in Austin
  • Restaurant in Chicago
  • Portfolio for a designer
  • E-commerce selling coffee beans
  • Dentist office in Miami
  • Personal blog about cooking

I opened all 7 tabs side by side.

And I froze.

They all looked like they were made by the same person.

Same hero section layout. Same button shapes. Same typography choices. Same "Wix vibe."

This isn't necessarily bad. Wix templates are professionally designed. They look good.

But they don't look like you. They look like Wix.

I showed my designer friend. She scrolled through the tabs and said:
"Yeah, those are all Wix sites. You can tell."

That's the tradeoff: Wix makes it easy to build a decent-looking site. But that site will look like a Wix site.

For some people, this doesn't matter. A yoga studio owner doesn't care if their site looks like Wix—they just want it to work.

But if brand identity matters? If you want to stand out? This is why I'd point you toward Framer or Webflow.

What I Wish I Knew Before Testing Wix

Would have saved me 12 hours of testing

1.

The "app store" is a money pit. Wix boasts 300+ apps, but most require paid subscriptions. Want a booking system? That's $15/mo extra. Need better forms? $10/mo. My "free" Wix site quickly became $80/mo with apps.

2.

Template switching destroys content. I picked a template, spent 4 hours adding content, then decided to try a different template. All my content disappeared. Wix doesn't migrate content between templates. This cost me half a day.

3.

The editor has TOO MANY options. I spent 2 hours looking for a simple spacing control. Wix's editor is powerful but overwhelming—like giving someone a Boeing 747 cockpit when they just want to drive to the grocery store.

4.

Mobile optimization is automatic but limited. Wix creates mobile versions automatically, which is great. But customizing the mobile view is frustratingly limited. I couldn't get mobile layouts to match my vision without desktop breaking.

How My Opinion Changed: The Hidden Cost Trap

From "easy and free" to "expensive and limited"

INITIAL IMPRESSION: "This is great! So easy, so many templates!"

First day with Wix, I built a decent-looking site in 2 hours. The drag-and-drop editor felt intuitive. I thought: "This is perfect for beginners." Initial score: 8.5/10.

THE REALITY HIT: "Wait... why is this $80/month?"

Week 2: I added a booking system ($15/mo), better forms ($10/mo), analytics upgrade ($8/mo), gallery pro ($6/mo). My "free" Wix site was now $79/month. And it still looked like a Wix site.

I lowered my score to 7.5/10. The value proposition collapsed.

FINAL VERDICT: "Good for absolute beginners. Bad for everyone else."

After a month, I settled at 8.3/10. Here's why: Wix IS easy. It DOES work. But it's a trap—sweet at first, expensive later, and you can never leave.

I stopped recommending Wix to anyone who cares about brand identity or long-term costs. It's only for people who need a site NOW and don't care about perfection.

Score evolution: 8.5 → 7.5 → 8.3. I bounced around because Wix has genuine strengths (ease of use) and genuine weaknesses (lock-in, hidden costs). The final score reflects: "Great for beginners, problematic for everyone else."

❌

Anyone who wants to own their code

Zero export option. Complete vendor lock-in. Use Framer or CodeDesign.ai instead.

❌

Performance-obsessed users

PageSpeed scores are mediocre (60-75 mobile). Framer scores 90-98.

❌

Budget-conscious long-term users

Apps add up fast. "Free" site becomes $50-80/mo with add-ons. Lock-in means keep paying or rebuild.

❌

People who switch templates often

Template switching destroys all content. Pick wrong = start over. This is a major pain point.

❌

Advanced e-commerce needs

Wix Stores is basic compared to WooCommerce (10Web) or Shopify. Limited inventory management.

If any of these are you: Consider Framer for design quality, 10Web for e-commerce, or Durable for speed.

Wix ADI: The AI Experience

Wix ADI (Artificial Design Intelligence) is Wix's answer to AI website building. You answer 6 questions about your business, preferences, and goals. Then Wix generates a complete website in under 2 minutes.

The good: It's incredibly fast. The questions are straightforward. You get a functional site quickly.

The bad: The designs feel generic. I tested ADI 5 times with different inputs and got sites that looked 80% similar. The AI optimizes for "safe" designs, not unique ones.

Vendor Lock-In: The Real Problem

Here's what nobody tells you about Wix: you cannot export your code.

I spent 52 hours building sites, only to realize if I ever want to leave Wix, I have to start from scratch. There's no HTML export. There's no WordPress migration. Your site lives on Wix servers, in Wix's proprietary format, forever.

Compare this to Framer (React export on Pro plan) or 10Web (WordPress-based). With Wix, you're renting your website, not owning it.

PageSpeed Performance

I ran Google PageSpeed tests on 5 Wix sites I created. The results were consistently mediocre:

  • Mobile: 60-75/100 (Needs improvement)
  • Desktop: 75-85/100 (Good but not great)
  • Main issue: Unused JavaScript and enormous DOM sizes

For comparison, Framer sites score 90-98/100. Durable sites score 85-95/100. Wix's feature-rich editor comes at a performance cost.

FAILURE #3: The Client Handoff Disaster

They were terrified of breaking something. Eventually stopped updating the site entirely because "Wix is too technical." For a tool that's supposed to be easy, this was a spectacular failure.

If they'd started with Shopify or WordPress, this would have been a $0 upgrade.
