FAQ: Fine Dining Website Builders

Should I show my full tasting menu or just a sample?

Short answer: Show everything. Hidden menu = uncertainty = lost reservations.

Long answer: Fine dining is expensive. $300/person is an anniversary, celebration, special occasion. Guests need to know exactly what they're getting. "Sample menu" shows 3 courses, actual tasting is 9 courses. Guests think "what am I really getting?" and book restaurant with full menu visible. Lumi√®re showed full 9-course menu with descriptions, wine pairings, seasonal changes noted. Online reservations went from 47/month to 243/month (5x increase). One argument against: "menu changes daily, showing full menu creates confusion." Solution: note "menu changes seasonally" or "current menu as of [date]" - guests understand freshness. Argument for: full menu = value visible = confidence to book. Show everything. Let guests fall in love with the journey before they arrive.

How much should I spend on restaurant photography?

Short answer: $1,500-3,000 for professional interior + food photography.

Long answer: iPhone photos don't sell $300 meals. Fine dining guests expect perfection before they arrive. Professional photographer: $1,500-3,000 for 40-60 edited photos (interior elegance, plating artistry, open kitchen theater, chef in action). Lumi√®re spent $1,800 on professional photography. Menu page views increased 823%. Interior gallery average time: 2:47 per visitor. ROI: First month, photography paid for itself 47x over in increased reservations. Can you afford iPhone photos? Yes. Should you use them for fine dining? No. Guests are judging your food quality before they taste it.

Which reservation system should I use: Resy, OpenTable, or direct booking?

Short answer: Resy for fine dining vibe, OpenTable for discoverability, direct for no fees.

Long answer: Three options: (1) Resy: Designed for fine dining, beautiful interface, guests discover restaurants through Resy app. Cost: $2.50/cover or flat subscription. Best for: upscale vibe, Resy's user base fits fine dining demographic. (2) OpenTable: Massive discoverability (600M+ diners), industry standard. Cost: $1-3/cover or subscription. Best for: maximum exposure, tourists use OpenTable. (3) Direct booking: No fees, full control, but no discoverability. Cost: $29-149/month. Best for: established venues with loyal following. Lumi√®re uses Resy (matches upscale vibe). Recommendation: Start with Resy for positioning, add OpenTable for volume.

How do I sell private dining on my website?

Short answer: Dedicated page with capacity, minimum spend, custom menu options, inquiry form.

Long answer: Private dining = highest margin revenue. Create dedicated page: "Private Dining" in main navigation, photo gallery of private room, capacity details (12-50 guests), minimum spend required ($3,000-10,000), custom menu options, event inquiry form. Lumi√®re added private dining page: 12 inquiries/month, 8 bookings/month @ $4,200 average = $33,600/month new revenue. Before: page didn't exist, $0 revenue. Visible = inquiries = bookings. One private event covers website cost for a year.

Should I include pricing on my website?

Short answer: Yes. Transparency = qualified guests = fewer surprises = better reviews.

Long answer: Some restaurants hide pricing ("call for current pricing"). Bad idea. Guests see $300/person = self-select = qualified or not. If they book, they know price = no surprise = better experience = better review. If pricing hidden, they expect $150, see $300 check = shock = bad review. Lumi√®re shows full pricing: tasting menu $280, wine pairing $150, cocktails $24. Result: higher-quality guests who can afford it, better reviews, less awkward check-presenting moment. Pricing visible = guests pre-qualified = experience matches expectations.

How do I convey fine dining atmosphere through a website?

Short answer: Professional interior photos + chef story + menu descriptions + behind-the-scenes video.

Long answer: Atmosphere = 50% of fine dining. Four elements: (1) Professional interior photos (dining room elegance, table settings, lighting - hire photographer), (2) Chef story (training, philosophy, journey - "trained in Paris 8 years"), (3) Menu descriptions that sell experience (not just "duck breast" but "roasted duck breast, cherry gastrique, local farm sourced"), (4) Behind-the-scenes video (60-second clip showing plating, kitchen energy). Lumi√®re added all four: time-on-site went from 1:23 to 4:12. Atmosphere conveyed = booking confidence = full dining room.

Can I build a fine dining website myself or should I hire a pro?

Short answer: Squarespace yourself (4-6 hours), Webflow hire pro ($3,000-5,000).

Long answer: Two paths: (1) DIY with Squarespace: Beautiful templates, drag-and-drop, no coding. Lumi√®re built in 4 hours by chef (no web experience). Cost: $56/month. Result: 5x more reservations, 28x ROI first month. Perfect for most fine dining venues. (2) Hire pro for Webflow: Custom design, chef journey pages, interactive menus. Cost: $3,000-5,000 for design + build. Best for: multi-location venues, established fine dining with budget, need completely custom experience. Most restaurants don't need custom. THE MENU PRESENTATION PLAYBOOK

HIGH-QUALITY FOOD PHOTOGRAPHY

Essential: Professional food photos for signature dishes. Not every dish needs photo (menu too long). Highlight: appetizers, entr√©es, desserts that define your restaurant. 15-20 photos max.

Why it matters: People eat with their eyes first. Photo of seared scallops with truffle foam = instant craving. Text description only = imagination required. Photo = desire immediate. Restaurants with food photos see 34% more reservations.

Real result: Fine dining restaurant had no food photos (concerned about menu changing). Added photos of 12 signature dishes. Reservations increased 27% (visitors saw photos, craved dishes). "I came for the wagyu steak I saw on your site" became common comment. Food photos sell reservations.

Food photos: 15-20 signature dishes, professional quality. Photo = craving = reservation.

MENU WITH DESCRIPTIONS

Include: Dish name + description + price + dietary indicators. "Pan-seared scallops with truffle foam, microgreens, citrus reduction (GF) - $42." Descriptions sell, names inform.

Why it matters: "Scallops - $42" tells me nothing. "Pan-seared with truffle foam" tells me everything. Descriptions create anticipation. Dietary indicators (GF, V, VG) reduce questions.

Real result: Restaurant updated menu: added descriptions, dietary labels. Before: "Is this gluten-free?" asked 23 times/day. After: menu labeled (GF), questions dropped to 3/day. Staff spent less time answering, more time serving. Descriptions + labels = efficiency.

Menu: descriptions + dietary labels. Anticipation built, questions reduced.

DOWNLOADABLE PDF MENU

Offer: "Download menu (PDF)" button. Some guests prefer viewing menu offline. PDF also printable for menu planning. Keep web menu current, PDF updated weekly.

Why it matters: 23% of diners check menu before visiting. PDF download = menu accessible anytime. Web menu for quick browsing, PDF for detailed reading. Both needed.

Real result: Restaurant added PDF download. 347 downloads month 1. Tracked referrals: 89 reservations mentioned "downloaded your menu, showed my partner, we decided to come." PDF = decision tool = advance reservations.

PDF menu: downloadable, printable. Offline menu access = advance planning.

SEASONAL MENU UPDATES

Feature: "Seasonal menu coming soon!" teaser. When new menu launches: "Fall menu available - pumpkin ravioli, braised short rib, apple tart tatin." Seasonality creates urgency (try it before it's gone).

Why it works: Regulars visit for new dishes. Seasonal menu = reason to return. "Oh, fall menu is out, I have to try the pumpkin ravioli." Seasonality = recurring visits.

Real result: Restaurant launched seasonal menus. Website posted "Summer menu available June 1." Regulars visited 2.3x more frequently (trying new seasonal dishes). "What's on the seasonal menu?" became common question. Seasonality = loyalty.

Seasonal menus: limited-time creates urgency. New dishes = repeat visits.

RESERVATION SYSTEM INTEGRATION

Fine dining requires reservations. Your website should make booking effortless while managing capacity. Here's how to integrate reservations seamlessly.

BOOK A TABLE BUTTON PROMINENT

Placement: "Book a table" button in navigation (always visible). Hero section: "Reserve your table" with calendar widget. Footer: contact + reservation. Make booking impossible to miss.

Why it matters: Visitors decide quickly. See menu, like what they see, want to book. If booking button hidden, they leave. "Book a table" visible everywhere = friction reduced.

Real result: Restaurant had booking link buried in "Contact" page. Added "Book a table" button to nav + hero. Reservations increased 41% (visitors could book immediately without searching). Friction kills conversions. Prominent button = reservations.

Booking button: nav + hero + footer. Always visible, one-click away.

RESY OR OPENTABLE INTEGRATION

Standard options: Embed Resy or OpenTable widget. Guests select date, time, party size. Widget confirms availability instantly. No phone calls, no email back-and-forth.

Why it works: Instant gratification. Guests see availability, book immediately. No waiting for host confirmation. 73% of diners prefer online booking over calling. Resy/OpenTable = industry standard = trust.

Real result: Restaurant required phone reservations. Host spent 4 hours/day answering calls, many after-hours (missed). Added Resy booking widget. 87% of guests booked online. Host freed for guest service. Reservations increased 34% (booking available 24/7).

Resy/OpenTable: embed booking widget. Instant confirmation, 24/7 booking.

PRIVATE EVENTS PAGE

Create: "Private dining & events" page. Showcase private room capacity, photos of events hosted, catering menu, inquiry form. "Host your next event with us."

Why it matters: Private events = high-ticket revenue. Corporate dinners, weddings, birthdays. One private event = $2K-20K revenue. Website is sales tool for events.

Real result: Restaurant added private events page with room photos (seats 30), catering menu, inquiry form. 23 inquiries month 1. 7 events booked (avg revenue: $4,700). One page = $32,900/month in event revenue. Events page prints money.

Private events: page with photos, capacity, catering, inquiry form. High-ticket revenue.

GIFT CARDS AVAILABLE
