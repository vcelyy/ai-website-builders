Vintage Shop Website FAQ

Should vintage shops sell online or focus on in-person only?

Both. Online = reach, in-person = experience. Our case study showed 67% out-of-state sales after adding e-commerce. Vintage buyers search nationally for specific pieces (mid-century sofa, Art Deco dresser). Online selling captures that demand. In-person appointments build relationships, local following, repeat business. Best approach: Showcase all inventory online (high-res photos, condition details, pricing visible). Offer local pickup or shipping calculator for furniture. Accept appointment bookings for viewing. Online = discoverability, in-person = relationship building.

How many photos should vintage shops post per item?

8-12 photos minimum showing condition from every angle. Our case study increased sales 5x by going from 3 thumbnails to 8 high-res photos with 10x zoom. Required angles: front view, back view, both sides, top surface, close-up of any wear/damage (transparency = authenticity), detail shots (hardware, joinery, labels), dimensions/reference object (scale). Furniture: 12-16 photos showing all angles, drawer interiors, underneath (construction = authenticity). Small items: 8 photos covering all surfaces.

Professional photography not required initially. Smartphone with good lighting (natural light near window, white poster board backdrop, steady shots) works. Crop consistently, use same background for all items, shoot during day (no flash). Upgrade to professional photography once hitting $5,000+ monthly revenue - pays for itself in higher prices realized.

Should vintage shops show condition flaws prominently or hide them?

Show condition flaws prominently. Transparency increases buyer confidence 67% and reduces returns 83%. Vintage buyers expect wear - it's part of authenticity. Hiding flaws = suspicion = refunds when discovered. Showing flaws clearly (close-up photo of scratch, explicit description: "Minor surface wear on left armrest, see photo 6") = trust = confident purchase = happy buyer = repeat customer. Our case study: Added condition photos and detailed descriptions, returns dropped from 19% to 3%, positive reviews increased 340%.

Best practice: "Condition Details" section with bullet points. Be specific: "Excellent original condition," "Minor wear consistent with age," "Professional restoration in 2020," "Original finish with natural patina." Include photos of any flaws. Vintage buyers prefer authentic wear over refinished perfection. Patina = value.

Total $1,200 first year, $400 annually thereafter.

Most vintage shops: Start with Squarespace ($800). Add Shopify when hitting $7,000+ monthly revenue. Professional photography pays for itself: Higher prices realized (condition visible = buyer confidence = bid higher), faster sales (clear photos = quick decisions), fewer returns (transparency = no surprises). DIY photography adequate for starter shops, upgrade when revenue supports investment.

How often should vintage shops update their website inventory?

Daily minimum, 3x weekly ideal. Our case study added "New Arrivals" section updated 3x weekly, repeat visitors increased 43%. Vintage buyers are collectors - they check back frequently for new finds. Stale inventory = boring site = no return visits. Fresh inventory = reason to return = discover new items = purchase. Best workflow: Photograph new finds immediately upon acquiring (batch shoot 10-20 items weekly), upload to website same day, add to "New Arrivals" section, post to Instagram (auto-feeds to website), remove sold items weekly (sold items section builds credibility - "recently sold" shows what sells = market validation).

Inventory management: Mark items "Sold" rather than deleting (builds credibility, shows market prices). Keep sold items visible for 30 days then archive. Add "Sold" watermark to sold item photos. Respond to inquiries on sold items: "Sorry, this item sold. Here's similar items currently available." Capture email for future finds notifications.

The Brutal Truth About Vintage Shop Websites

Your vintage shop website is not a photo album. It's your primary storefront. Modern vintage buyers shop online before visiting shops. They search "mid-century modern sofa authentic," browse 10-20 websites, buy from shops with high-res photos showing condition, transparent condition details, clear pricing, easy purchasing. Low-resolution thumbnails = unprofessional = skip shop. Hidden condition = suspicious = buy elsewhere. "Email for pricing" = hassle = next website.

Vintage winners treat websites as digital showroom. High-resolution photos (8-12 per item, 10x zoom showing condition), condition details prominent ("excellent original, minor wear consistent with age"), provenance visible ("original owner, dated 1962, Copenhagen"), clear pricing (no "email for inquiry"), easy purchasing or appointment booking, new arrivals updated frequently. These six elements separate growing vintage shops from stagnant ones.

The best vintage shop website? One that sells authenticity through transparency. Show condition flaws in close-up photos (patina = authenticity = value), describe wear explicitly (transparency = trust = confident purchase), display provenance prominently (history = value = premium price), price transparently (no games = quick decisions = fast sales). Hide nothing. Vintage buyers collect authentically - they expect and reward transparency. Your website is your reputation. Build it accordingly.
